Dynamically Predicting Used Vehicle Prices in the California Car Market

A statistical Machine Learning Study by Sahand Shafieha, Some Guy Who Loves Cars & Rock N' Roll.

What Are We Doing Here?

All I did was I collected some data from MarketCheck API (who are apparently pretty good at reporting used car sales data), cleaned it up, and trained some statistical models to perform the prediction.

What is a statistical model, you would ask? In simple terms, models help us determine the best line of fitment to our data to analyze trends, which help us predict data. Then I thought, why not make this dynamic so it automatically fetches some data once a week, performs the cleaning, and trains the data without me having to do anything? Therefore I did that too.

If you're a nerd and care about what models I trained, they were four independent Random Forest models, and each model was evaluated on a held-out test set using RMSE, MAE, MAPE, and R².

How Did I Conducted The Prediction?

Data Cleaning & Imputation in Simplest Terms

Missing data is a common artifact of API scraping. We used K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Imputation to fill in gaps for mileage and price. By comparing similar vehicles, we ensured our dataset remained robust without losing valuable rows of data.

Market Stratification & Handling Categorical Data

Vehicles exhibit significantly different depreciation dynamics depending on their market tier. To capture these separate price regimes, we split the data into Affordable, Mid-Range, and Luxury tiers based on real-world California market benchmarks:

Affordable Tier

Under $30,000: Grounded in California's average used car transaction price benchmark (~$29k–$31k). Captures standard entry-level, budget-conscious models.

Mid-Range Tier

$30,000 – $45,000: Captures mainstream mid-size SUVs, popular crossovers, and entry-level luxury vehicles.

Luxury Tier

Over $45,000: Encompasses high-end premium vehicles, performance models, and statistical outliers at the upper end of the market.

Handling High-Cardinality Categorical Data

To prepare categorical features for our Machine Learning models, we applied a structured preprocessing pipeline:

  • ✔ Factor Standardization: Cleaned whitespace and normalized strings to uppercase (e.g., standardizing car makes and body types).
  • ✔ Body Type Aggregation: Grouped complex NHTSA VIN decoder body styles into 8 simplified categories (SUV, Sedan, Pickup, Hatchback, Coupe, Convertible, Van, Wagon) plus a minor category default to reduce complexity.
  • ✔ High-Cardinality Collapse: Collapsed car makes representing less than 2% market share into an "Other" category to prevent model overfitting.
  • ✔ One-Hot Dummy Encoding: Converted these factors into binary indicator columns, with built-in zero-variance filters and novel level protection for future predictions.
Random Forest Engine

This one is easy! Our predictions are powered by a Random Forest Regressor. It looks at hundreds of decision trees to find the most accurate price based on mileage and market demand.
